# Freedom

> *"On Freedom, no exile is burdened by the concerns of their former life."*
>
> — Union Penal Authority Doctrine

**Freedom** is a penal world of the Confederate Vanguard Union and one of the largest sources of strategic minerals in Union territory. Feared throughout the Core, the planet serves as both a place of permanent exile and a critical source of labor and military manpower.

Those sentenced to Freedom lose their citizenship, titles, inheritance rights, political privileges, and legal ties to their former lives. Once transferred to the planet, they belong to Freedom for the remainder of their lives.

To critics, Freedom is little more than a prison world.

To its defenders, it is a system through which those who have failed the Union may repay their debt through service. Union officials often describe Freedom not as a punishment, but as a form of liberation—a place where individuals are stripped of the distractions, ambitions, and obligations of their former lives and given a single purpose: service.

## History

Freedom was founded during the Great Restoration as a remote detention world used to hold prisoners of war captured during the conflict. Rich deposits of strategic minerals quickly made the planet economically valuable, and the Union soon expanded the original camps into large-scale mining operations worked primarily by prisoner labor.

As the war continued, military authorities began drawing manpower directly from the prison population. What began as ad hoc penal battalions gradually evolved into the Freedom Corps, a permanent military organization tasked with frontier security, hazardous operations, and other assignments deemed unsuitable for conventional forces.

Early Freedom Corps units suffered catastrophic casualty rates. Equipment was often outdated, missions were dangerous, and commanders viewed the exiles as expendable. Over time, however, the harsh conditions produced a distinct military culture that emphasized obedience, endurance, sacrifice, and loyalty above all else. Those unable or unwilling to adapt rarely survived.

Following the Great Restoration, the Union retained the Freedom system rather than dismantling it. Criminals, deserters, insurgents, failed officials, debtors, and political dissidents gradually replaced the original prisoners of war. Mining operations expanded, military installations multiplied, and Freedom developed into a permanent institution within Union society.

Over the centuries, several Freedom Corps officers rose from exile to achieve fame through extraordinary acts of service. Their stories remain a common feature of Union propaganda, presented as proof that even those who have lost everything may still find purpose through service to the Union.

Today, Freedom stands as both a warning and an ideal. To some, it is the harshest symbol of Union authority. To others, it represents one of the Union's oldest beliefs: that a person's worth is measured not by what they possess, but by what they are willing to sacrifice.

## Sentencing to Freedom

Exile to Freedom is among the harshest punishments available within the Confederate Vanguard Union.

Individuals sentenced to Freedom permanently lose their status as Union citizens. Noble titles are stripped away. Property and inheritance claims are voided. Political offices are forfeited. Existing military commissions are revoked.

Upon arrival, all exiles become servants of Freedom.

Former identities carry little weight on the planet. Criminals, failed nobles, disgraced officers, political dissidents, debtors, and deserters often find themselves living side-by-side within the same settlements.

Union doctrine teaches that exile frees an individual from the burdens of status, wealth, family expectations, and personal ambition. Critics view this as little more than political rhetoric. Supporters argue that only on Freedom can a person be judged solely by their willingness to serve.

Among Freedom's inhabitants, it is often said:

> *"Freedom receives no nobles, no officers, no criminals. Only exiles."*

## The Two Paths

Every inhabitant of Freedom ultimately serves through one of two institutions: the Mines or the Freedom Corps.

### The Mines

The majority of exiles spend their lives in the mines.

Freedom contains vast deposits of strategic minerals that are essential to Union industry and military production. Mining operations stretch across entire continents and are notorious for dangerous working conditions, industrial accidents, violent labor disputes, and brutal environmental hazards.

Life expectancy within many mining districts is short. The work is exhausting, dangerous, and often lethal. Yet despite their reputation, the mines remain one of the most important economic assets in the Union.

### The Freedom Corps

The Freedom Corps serves as the military arm of Freedom.

Recruitment is drawn almost entirely from the world's exile population. The Corps is not designed to produce elite soldiers. Instead, it produces obedient, resilient, and expendable troops capable of operating in conditions that would break conventional military formations.

The training process is notoriously brutal. Attrition rates are high, discipline is severe, and failure is rarely tolerated. Those who survive develop a reputation for extraordinary endurance and unwavering loyalty.

Freedom Corps units are rarely deployed to the Union's major worlds and are almost never entrusted with critical military operations against rival Great Houses. Instead, they are typically assigned to remote frontier settlements, isolated border garrisons, pirate suppression campaigns, anti-bandit operations, and other dangerous postings where casualties are expected and reinforcements are often far away.

Among the regular military, the Corps is frequently viewed as a force of last resort—useful, reliable, and ultimately expendable. Yet despite this reputation, Freedom Corps soldiers have defended countless frontier communities and held some of the Union's most isolated outposts for generations.

## Culture of Service

Life on Freedom revolves around service.

Unlike the rest of the Union, where military service is viewed as a civic duty, the inhabitants of Freedom possess no path back to ordinary citizenship. Their former lives are gone.

For many, service becomes the only remaining source of purpose, status, or identity.

This culture has produced a reputation for fanaticism among both miners and soldiers. Loyalty is valued above talent, and commitment is valued above ambition.

Within the Freedom Corps, individuals who attempt to exploit the system for personal gain are viewed with particular contempt. Veterans often place greater value on reliability and sacrifice than on battlefield skill.

Many Freedom Corps soldiers openly embrace the belief that service itself is the highest form of redemption available to them. Among the lower classes of the Union, a small number of volunteers even choose to travel to Freedom willingly, believing that a life spent in service on the world is preferable to a life of failure elsewhere. Such volunteers are rare, but their existence reflects the deep influence of Union ideals regarding duty, sacrifice, and purpose.

---

## Reputation

Freedom occupies a strange place within Union society.

Most citizens fear it. Many military officers mock it. Politicians invoke it. Parents threaten their children with it. Yet despite its reputation, few deny its importance.

The mines fuel Union industry. The Freedom Corps guards some of the Union's most dangerous frontiers. Generations of exiles have shed blood in service to a nation that no longer considers them citizens.

For some, the planet's name is a bitter irony. For others, it reflects a distinctly Union belief: that true freedom is found not through personal choice, but through absolute dedication to a purpose greater than oneself.

As a result, Freedom is simultaneously despised, feared, respected, and relied upon.

---

## Legacy

Throughout Union history, a small number of Freedom Corps officers have risen from exile to become legendary military figures. Their stories are frequently celebrated in Union propaganda as examples of redemption through service.

Critics note that for every celebrated hero, millions have died anonymously in the mines or on distant frontier worlds.

Supporters argue that Freedom represents one of the Union's most fundamental beliefs:

> *A citizen may lose their rights, their wealth, and their status.*
>
> *They never lose the opportunity to serve.*
