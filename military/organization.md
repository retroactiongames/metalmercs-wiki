> — Union field doctrine

The military structure of the Core is built upon a long tradition of hierarchical command, strategic coordination, and mechanized warfare. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Although heavily modernized over centuries of conflict, most military systems throughout civilized space still retain organizational foundations dating back to the earliest eras of mech warfare.

Modern military doctrine emphasizes:
- clear chains of command
- operational flexibility
- rapid deployment
- mechanized coordination
- scalable force structure

This system allows military forces ranging from:
- small mercenary units
- frontier militias
- House expeditionary forces
- massive interstellar commands

to coordinate effectively across enormous theaters of war.

At the center of nearly all modern military organization stands the mech.

## Structure and Hierarchy

Military organization throughout the Core is based upon nested command structures in which each level controls several subordinate formations. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

This hierarchy exists to provide:
- battlefield clarity
- operational discipline
- strategic scalability
- logistical coordination

The system also allows commanders to rapidly reorganize units depending upon:
- terrain
- mission profile
- attrition
- deployment limitations
- political considerations

Although every major faction maintains unique command traditions and rank titles, the underlying organizational structure remains broadly recognizable throughout civilized space.

## The Column

The fundamental operational unit of modern mech warfare is the *Column*. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

A standard Column consists of:
- four mechs
- one commanding officer
- supporting technical staff
- logistical attachments

Columns are typically commanded by a captain or equivalent junior field officer depending upon factional doctrine.

The Column is designed to function as a highly flexible battlefield element capable of:
- reconnaissance
- assault operations
- patrol work
- urban combat
- defensive action
- independent deployment

Because of its versatility, the Column forms the foundation of nearly all military operations throughout the Core.

Among mech pilots, the phrase *“holding the line with four steel”* is a common reference to the importance of the Column structure.

## Reinforced Columns

In high-risk or specialized operations, militaries may deploy Reinforced Columns consisting of:
- six mechs
- occasionally eight mechs

These formations are especially common among:
- mercenary companies
- frontier militias
- assault specialists
- irregular warfare groups

Reinforced Columns sacrifice some mobility and logistical efficiency in exchange for increased tactical flexibility and battlefield endurance.

## Companies

Three Columns are typically organized into a Company. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

A standard Company fields:
- twelve mechs
- dedicated logistical support
- tactical coordination staff
- maintenance personnel

Companies represent the smallest formation capable of sustained independent combat operations.

Many mercenary companies derive their name from this organizational level, though actual mercenary force sizes vary enormously.

Reinforced Companies may field:
- sixteen to twenty mechs
- additional logistics support
- specialist units

depending upon operational doctrine.

## Wards

Several Companies combine to form a *Ward*. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Wards generally consist of:
- forty to fifty mechs
- command support staff
- integrated logistics networks
- coordinated battlefield doctrine

Wards serve as the backbone of most large-scale military operations.

They possess enough force projection capability to:
- secure territory
- defend strategic regions
- conduct major offensives
- hold urban sectors

Many military historians consider the Ward the smallest truly strategic mech formation.

## Marches

Several Wards combine into a *March*. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Marches usually field:
- one hundred to two hundred mechs
- extensive logistical infrastructure
- mobile command elements
- aerospace support attachments

Marches are capable of:
- sustained planetary campaigns
- regional occupation
- major breakthrough operations
- prolonged attritional warfare

In frontier conflicts, the deployment of a full March is often viewed as evidence that a conflict has escalated into a major military crisis.

## Brigades, Divisions, and Corps

At the upper levels of military organization, formations scale into truly enormous mechanized armies. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

These formations include:

### Brigade
- 1,000–2,000 mechs
- 7–10 Marches

### Division
- approximately 10,000 mechs
- 4–5 Brigades

### Corps
- approximately 50,000–75,000 mechs
- 5–7 Divisions

### Command
- approximately 200,000–300,000 mechs
- 3–6 Corps

These immense formations require:
- planetary-scale logistics
- fleet coordination
- industrial support
- strategic command infrastructure

Few modern conflicts involve full Command-level deployments due to their staggering economic and logistical requirements.

## The Scale of Modern Warfare

Despite the enormous numbers associated with upper-level formations, true Command-scale warfare is exceedingly rare in the modern Core.

The cost of:
- mech maintenance
- transport logistics
- reactor fuel
- ammunition production
- fleet support

makes prolonged large-scale warfare extraordinarily difficult.

As a result, many modern conflicts revolve around:
- limited offensives
- proxy warfare
- mercenary intervention
- strategic raids
- territorial disputes

rather than total war.

## Faction-Specific Hierarchies

Although military organization remains broadly standardized, each major faction utilizes distinct command titles rooted in its own history and culture. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

### Omnisphere Imperium

The Imperium maintains some of the oldest surviving military traditions in civilized space.

Imperium titles are believed to predate even the Old Empire itself.

Their hierarchy emphasizes:
- ancient duty
- guardianship
- continuity
- martial tradition

### Confederate Vanguard Union

The Union uses highly modernized military terminology emphasizing:
- operational clarity
- efficiency
- battlefield coordination

Due to the Union’s military influence, Union command terminology is especially common among mercenary organizations.

### Helios Sovereignty

The Sovereignty blends military command with aristocratic tradition.

Many officer titles possess noble or ceremonial connotations reflecting the faction’s dynastic culture.

### Starcrest Protectorate

Protectorate command structure emphasizes:
- defense
- guardianship
- territorial stewardship

Their titles often reflect protective or martial symbolism.

### Orion Corporate

Corporate military structure blends traditional military command with administrative and industrial terminology.

Corporate command philosophy emphasizes:
- coordination
- systems management
- operational integration

## Mercenary Adaptations

Mercenary organizations frequently modify traditional military structures to suit operational requirements. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Common adaptations include:
- reinforced columns
- irregular company sizes
- modular command structures
- temporary battlefield formations
- contract-specific task groups

Greater Mercenary Companies often develop highly sophisticated command systems rivaling those of the Great Houses themselves.

Meanwhile, smaller mercenary outfits may operate with extremely informal hierarchies built more around:
- reputation
- battlefield experience
- pilot seniority
- contractual authority

than formal military tradition.

## Combined Arms Warfare

Although mech formations dominate modern military doctrine, successful campaigns require extensive coordination between:
- mechs
- infantry
- aerospace forces
- artillery
- fleet assets
- logistics divisions

Modern battlefield doctrine increasingly emphasizes integrated combined-arms operations rather than pure mech superiority alone.

Even the most powerful mech formations remain vulnerable without adequate support infrastructure.

## Cultural Importance

Military rank and organizational structure remain deeply embedded within the identity of the Core.

Across civilized space, military terminology influences:
- politics
- mercenary culture
- civilian administration
- industrial organization
- social prestige

Veteran officers often carry enormous political influence long after active service ends.

Many House leaders themselves possess military backgrounds or ceremonial military titles.

Throughout much of the Core, military hierarchy is viewed not merely as organizational necessity —

but as one of the foundational structures preserving civilization itself.

## Modern Outlook

As instability rises throughout the Core and frontier regions alike, military organizations continue adapting to the realities of modern warfare.

Advancements in:
- mech coordination
- neural networking
- battlefield communication
- electronic warfare
- logistics management

are gradually reshaping traditional command structures.

Even so, the ancient hierarchy remains remarkably resilient.

From the smallest four-mech Column to the vast Commands that marshal hundreds of thousands of war machines, the military structure of the Core continues to reflect a civilization built upon organized steel, disciplined command, and the constant shadow of war.
