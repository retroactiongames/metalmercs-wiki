# Mechs of the Core

> *“Every House claims its own doctrine. Every battlefield still speaks the same language: steel.”*  
> — StarCom tactical archive

This page catalogs the commonly recognized mech designs fielded by the Great Houses of the Core. It is a roster-focused companion to the broader [Mechs](../mechs/) overview.

### Hornet `HN-120`

One of the oldest light designs still in circulation, the Hornet was cheap to manufacture and simple to maintain. Armed with twin light lasers, it was never meant to duel, but rather to scout, harass, and sting at vulnerable points before fleeing. Its proliferation across the Core is owed to its reliability and low cost—where there is conflict, Hornets are never far behind.

### Lynx `LNX-40E`

The Lynx became popular during the border wars for its balance of mobility and firepower. With paired medium lasers and a small rocket rack, it could serve as both flanker and support unit. Though newer mechs have surpassed it, the Lynx endures thanks to its rugged frame and versatility—qualities that made it a staple in every House’s arsenal.

### Outrider `OR-640`

Built for patrol duty and frontier policing, the Outrider combined endurance with straightforward weaponry. Its heavy and medium lasers gave it enough punch to deter raiders without requiring complex supply lines. While unimpressive by modern standards, the Outrider’s long service life and adaptability have ensured its presence in garrisons across the Core.

### Yeoman `YM-10F`

The Yeoman was designed as a dependable multi-role platform for Houses lacking specialized production. Its mixed loadout—autoguns, lasers, and scorchers—made it a jack-of-all-trades capable of handling infantry, armor, or other mechs in a pinch. Though rarely a commander’s first choice, the Yeoman’s steady performance earned it a reputation as the soldier’s friend.

### Lineman `LM-30C`

Heavy, slow, and built to endure punishment, the Lineman was conceived as a frontline brawler to hold the line while others maneuvered. Its vulkan cannon and paired autoguns provided steady suppressive fire. Though outclassed by newer heavies, it remains common wherever Houses need durable firepower to anchor defenses.

### Lancer `LNC-220`

The Lancer was among the first medium-heavies to emphasize precision energy fire over massed rockets. Armed with a heavy laser and layered support beams, it excelled at punching through weakened armor. Once hailed as a duelist’s machine, it is now more often used as a training platform or second-line support, though many veteran pilots still prize its accuracy.

### Longstrike `LG-210`

An aging missile platform, the Longstrike was originally deployed to provide sustained bombardment from behind the lines. With its paired racks and autogun backups, it could soften enemy positions before an assault. Though slow and vulnerable up close, its simplicity and ease of production have kept it in steady circulation across the Core.

### Champion `CH-70K`

Once built to serve in ceremonial tournaments as well as war, the Champion balanced missile fire with lasers and a vulkan. Though never as specialized as later duelists, it gained popularity as a reliable all-rounder. Many Houses still field Champions in honor guards or training battalions, where their long heritage and recognizable silhouette command respect.

### Bulldog `BD-770`

The Bulldog earned its name in countless sieges, battering defenses with high energy cannons and rocket salvos. Slow but stubborn, it excelled in drawn-out attrition battles. Even today, the Bulldog remains a favored choice for militias and second-line forces who need a hard-hitting mech that can take punishment and keep advancing.

### Marksman `MK-350`

The Marksman was designed as a fire-support chassis, mounting multiple vulkans for sustained barrages. Though criticized for its heat inefficiency and limited armor, it carved a niche as a battlefield “sweeper,” punishing light mechs and infantry that strayed too close. In modern times, it is often relegated to defensive roles, but its name still carries weight.

### Gladiator `GL-882`

A relic of the arena era, the Gladiator was built for spectacle as much as war. With rockets, vulkan, and lasers, it was capable of delivering flashy, punishing blows that made it a crowd favorite. Many Houses retired theirs to ceremonial duty, but in poorer regions of the Core, Gladiators still see combat, proving that even showpieces can draw blood.

### Kingsguard `KG-950`

The Kingsguard was once marketed as the ultimate “command mech,” a prestige unit to accompany noble lords into battle. Its missiles and mixed weaponry made it flexible, while its armor kept its pilot secure. Though time has dulled its reputation, the Kingsguard endures as a symbol of authority on the battlefield—still favored by militia leaders and House scions alike.

### Raptor `RPT-1R`

Developed as a fast-response raider during the Union’s early frontier wars, the Raptor sacrifices heavy armor for speed and strike capability. Its rocket pods and light lasers made it ideal for harassing supply lines and crippling softer targets before heavier Union assets arrived. Though lightly protected, pilots trusted its agility and ferocity—earning the name Raptor for its swift, predatory strikes.

### Vagabond `VG-7H`

The Vagabond was born of necessity during the Exile, when scattered Union units required a versatile scout and skirmisher. Outfitted with jump jets and a mixed ballistic-energy loadout, it excels in rough terrain where heavier mechs cannot tread. Its name reflects both its wandering origins and its reputation as a survivor, roaming far from supply lines and returning with kills to prove its worth.

### Slugger `SLG-1A`

Designed for brutal city fighting during the Restoration, the Slugger carries a heavy energy cannon backed by close-support lasers. Slow but unyielding, it was built to clear enemy strongpoints building by building. The name Slugger is both a nod to its hammering firepower and the Union’s reputation for winning through attrition, no matter how long the fight drags on.

### Alpha Wolf `AWLF-5K`

The Alpha Wolf represents the Union’s doctrine of coordinated pack warfare. Armed with paired autoguns, lasers, and missiles, it was designed to anchor wolfpack formations that overwhelmed isolated foes through relentless firepower. Feared for its ability to tear through both armor and morale, the Alpha Wolf earned its name by always leading the kill.

### Crossfire `CRF-0S`

Born from Union doctrine that no enemy should leave the field unscathed, the Crossfire bristles with overlapping weapon arcs—Vulkan cannon, rockets, lasers, and autogun. It was intended for siege and suppression, saturating kill zones until nothing remained. Pilots often joked that standing in its line of fire was like being caught between two armies—hence the name Crossfire.

### Firebrand `FR-3N`

The Firebrand’s origin lies in the Union’s scorched-earth campaigns. Armed with an array of Scorchers supported by lasers and autoguns, it was built to terrorize entrenched enemies, burning them from cover and leaving nothing but ash. The Union embraced the mech’s fiery symbolism, fielding it as both weapon and warning: resistance would be answered with flames.

### Dragoon `DGN-8S`

The Dragoon was designed as a flexible heavy line unit, able to hold or advance with equal confidence. Combining a heavy energy cannon, vulkan, and defensive lasers, it proved especially valuable in prolonged campaigns where reliability outweighed elegance. Its name recalls the mounted infantry of old—soldiers who could fight wherever they were needed, a tradition the Union holds dear.

### Maverick `MAV-8K`

The Maverick was developed for commanders who demanded independence from supply trains. Its rockets and high energy cannon made it a devastating ambush platform, while its jump jets allowed it to strike from unexpected angles. The name Maverick honors both its battlefield role and the defiant Union spirit of breaking from convention to seize victory.

### Jackman `JCK-77`

The Jackman was built during the urban reclamations, where Union forces needed a close-range brawler to clear streets and hold chokepoints. Armed with paired vulkans and heavy lasers, it is devastating in knife-fight ranges. The name reflects the laborers who “jackhammered” the Union’s cities back into shape, embodying relentless industrial grit turned to war.

### Patriot `PTR-007`

The Patriot is perhaps the most symbolic of Union mechs, a walking fortress embodying their doctrine of sacrifice and resilience. With thick armor, broad weapon coverage, and missile support, it became the backbone of ceremonial as well as battlefield formations. Its name was no accident—the Union intended it as a rallying banner, a machine that stood unbroken while others fell.

### Hammerhead `HMR-4T`

Developed for shipboard and port assaults, the Hammerhead mounts twin heavy energy cannons backed by autoguns and lasers. Its name derives from its brutal frontal assault style, “hammering” through defenses like the predator of the sea. Pilots often describe it as inelegant but unstoppable—exactly the qualities the Union prizes.

### Challenger `CHL-4R`

The Challenger was created during the Union’s dueling trials, where mechs were fielded to prove dominance over rival Houses. Mixing rockets, autoguns, and lasers, it was designed for versatility in single combat as well as battlefield challenges. Though less specialized than some designs, its name and legacy are steeped in Union pride: to challenge is to overcome.

### Brigadier `BRG-88R`

Built to lead batteries of missile carriers, the Brigadier became the cornerstone of Union siege doctrine. Capable of drowning entrenched foes in waves of rockets and missiles, it earned a reputation for ending battles before they truly began. The name Brigadier reflects its command role—always at the center of Union formations, directing fire and breaking lines.

### Bulwark `BLW-5K`

Designed as a defensive anchor, the Bulwark carries massive armor and layered weaponry suited for holding ground against superior numbers. In campaigns where the Union was forced to defend its hard-won territories, the Bulwark became both shield and hammer. Its name speaks for itself: immovable, unshakable, a wall of steel.

### Sword Breaker `SBRK-7R`

The Sword Breaker was engineered specifically to counter the dueling champions of rival Houses. With heavy vulkans and precision medium lasers, it excelled at stripping weapons from enemy mechs and leaving them powerless. Its name is both literal and symbolic—the Union’s message that no foreign blade, however sharp, could pierce their resolve.

### Punisher `PNR-004`

The Punisher was born from campaigns of retribution. With a heavy energy cannon, ultra vulkan, and missile support, it was built to chase down and annihilate those who dared defy Union authority. Slow but merciless, the Punisher lives up to its name, serving as executioner on the battlefield where no mercy is given.

### Gallimagus `GLM-8G`

The Gallimagus is an ancient design, said to descend from pre-Exile Empire prototypes. Mounting an unprecedented four heavy energy cannons, it was rarely produced due to cost, but every unit became a battlefield legend. Its name, drawn from old Union sagas, evokes a monstrous beast of war—rare, mythical, and terrifying in its wrath.

### Liberator `LBR-9D`

Developed during the Long Exile by remnants of the fallen Empire, the Liberator was designed to spearhead counteroffensives and break enemy sieges. It played a decisive role in the Great Restoration, leading the charge to reclaim the Core worlds. Adopted as the Union’s premiere assault mech, it endures as both battlefield powerhouse and enduring symbol of liberation.

### Messenger `MSG-1R`

A swift harasser and scout, the Messenger relies on jump jets and missile bursts to unsettle lighter foes. Its role is to deliver a clear message on the battlefield—that Sovereignty forces are advancing, and their arrival is inevitable.

### Icarus `ICS-3G`

The Icarus embodies Sovereignty daring and hubris—fast, jump-capable, and armed with rockets for dramatic strikes. Though lightly armored, its theatrics earned it glory in countless tournaments. Pilots know the cautionary tale of its name, but many still fly it recklessly, chasing the sun of renown.

### Pegasus `PGS-H`

Inspired by myth, the Pegasus was designed as an aerial duelist, its jump jets giving it a graceful leap few others could match. Bristling with light and medium lasers, it dances around heavier foes, striking with elegance. In parades as in battle, the Pegasus is prized as a symbol of Sovereignty’s radiant majesty.

### Sentry `SN-21`

The Sentry was built for watchful dominance, its long-range high energy cannon and missile banks intended to hold borders in clear view. It lacks mobility, but its very presence is a deterrent, projecting Sovereignty vigilance. Many noble estates still keep a Sentry posted at their gates, a statue of steel as much as a guardian.

### Warden `WRD-N1`

Where the Sentry watches, the Warden intervenes. Agile for its size, it was built to guard noble households and convoy routes. Its heavy laser and missiles strike with authority, while its name reinforces Sovereignty propaganda: every subject is safe under noble protection—so long as they remain loyal.

### Lamplighter `LLT-6Y`

A mech named for illumination, the Lamplighter was created to pierce darkness both literal and symbolic. Its long-range energy cannon and array of lasers allow it to light up a battlefield from afar. To the Sovereignty, deploying Lamplighters is an act of enlightenment, burning away shadows of rebellion with radiant fire.

### Legionnaire `LGN-A`

The Legionnaire was mass-produced during Sovereignty campaigns to enforce order in the Core. Fitted with a medium MAC, missiles, and lasers, it is a versatile soldier’s machine. The name recalls the legions of ancient empires—disciplined, unstoppable, and loyal to the Sovereignty above all else.

### Paladin `PLDN-00C`

A mech wrapped in religious and chivalric symbolism, the Paladin was designed to embody the Sovereignty’s holy mandate. Rockets for righteous fury, lasers for guiding light, and a MAC to deliver judgment. In battle, it is as much a sermon as a weapon, reminding allies and enemies alike of the Sovereignty’s divine right to rule.

### Herald `HRL-6D`

Where nobles feared assassination or insurrection, Heralds were deployed. Heavy armor and powerful missile barrages made them excellent protectors of VIPs and sacred sites. To this day, Sovereignty rulers keep Heralds in their personal retinues, their name a constant reminder of paternal care—and veiled threat.

### Protector `PRO-T1`

The Protector was created as a battlefield shield for Sovereignty banners. Mixing heavy lasers with rockets and missiles, it can blunt enemy advances while rallying allies. Noble houses often decorate Protectors with gilded heraldry, ensuring that their role as both bulwark and symbol is unmistakable.

### Dark Knight `DK-2R`

The Dark Knight carries an aura of dread deliberately cultivated by Sovereignty engineers. Long-range energy weapons paired with a heavy laser give it punishing reach, while its sinister name and black-lacquered armor turn it into an icon of fear. Where the Dark Knight walks, rebellion tends to wither.

### Excalibur `EX-3R`

Few names carry more weight than Excalibur, and the Sovereignty bestowed it upon this duelist’s chassis. Armed with MACs, lasers, and rockets, it was designed for decisive, theatrical victories. Excaliburs often lead tournament fields, their pilots draped in noble colors, striking as much for honor as for conquest.

### Lionheart `LHR-5T`

The Lionheart epitomizes Sovereignty nobility: powerful, courageous, and unflinching. With heavy lasers, rockets, and long-range cannons, it dominates battlefields while bearing crests of golden lions. More than a weapon, it is a living coat of arms, proof that Sovereignty bloodlines rule by strength and valor.

### Lancelot `LNC-10T`

The Lancelot was built to combine elegance with battlefield dominance. Its MAC and missile systems give it both reach and crushing power, while its lasers keep foes at bay. No Sovereignty parade is complete without a Lancelot striding at the fore, the paragon of chivalric warfare.

### Great Sword `SWRD-001`

The Great Sword is the Sovereignty’s answer to the Union’s brute heavies: less subtle, more theatrical. Quad MACs and paired heavy lasers strike with the force of a descending blade. Its very silhouette evokes a weapon raised for the killing blow, a message that Sovereignty justice is swift and merciless.

### Colossus `COL-U5`

Massive and imposing, the Colossus is a walking fortress designed to awe as much as destroy. Mounting a heavy MAC alongside multiple lasers, it crushes resistance under overwhelming firepower. Wherever a Colossus is deployed, it is as much a proclamation as an army—Sovereignty power manifest in steel.

### Monarch `MON-A7`

The Monarch was never just a war machine—it was designed as a throne of iron for Sovereignty lords. With missile arrays and heavy lasers, it wields devastating power while carrying the regalia of its house. To see a Monarch on the field is to witness Sovereignty authority embodied, its very name leaving no doubt of who rules.

### Olympian `OLYM-D`

The Olympian is the pinnacle of Sovereignty design philosophy: mythic in name, majestic in presence, and terrible in power. With MACs, long-range cannons, missiles, and lasers, it commands every battlefield range. Sovereignty propaganda hails it as proof of their divine heritage—an Olympian god made metal, standing above all mortal challengers.

### War Dog `WAR-D1`

One of the oldest fielded designs in the Protectorate arsenal, the War Dog earned its name in decades of border wars where packs of them ran down raiders under cover of smoke. Reliable and loyal in function, it remains a cornerstone of Protectorate doctrine.

### Reaver `RVR-N5`

First deployed during the Black Hills Campaign, the Reaver became infamous for tearing through lighter mechs with its crushing fist and rockets. Its brutal reputation lingers; to Protectorate troops, a Reaver on the field is a promise of broken lines.

### Jackal `JCK-L4`

Known as the “stormcrow” of the Protectorate, the Harbinger often appears at the outset of offensives, signaling devastation to come. Its combination of fists, vulkan, and lasers makes it a herald of annihilation as much as a front-line bruiser.

### Harbinger `HAR-G9`

Named for its role as a herald of destruction, the Harbinger combines brute force with relentless suppressive fire. Its battle fist and vulkan cannon hammer enemies at close range, while laser support ensures there is no safe angle of approach.

### Beast `BST-R3`

The Beast traces back to Protectorate shock-divisions raised from penal battalions. Pilots dubbed it for its savage ultra-vulkan recoil and feral silhouette, and the name stuck. Today it remains a terror weapon, more feared than refined.

### Instigator `INS-G9`

The Instigator is a line-breaker, built to provoke chaos. Its heavy cannon and rocket payload drive enemies from cover, while laser fire harasses stragglers. True to its name, it starts fights the Protectorate intends to finish.

### Razorback `RZK-7B`

With its distinctive armored ridges, the Razorback resembles the boar for which it is named. It first entered service during the Dust Marches, where its heavy autogun and laser battery carved corridors through smoke-choked canyons.

### Neanderthal `NEA-R8`

The Neanderthal earned its name during testing, where its sheer, blunt brutality demolished mock fortresses in minutes. It remains the Protectorate’s premier siege breaker, a mech that embodies unrefined but devastating power.

### Barbarian `BAR-N8`

The Barbarian embodies the Protectorate’s doctrine of raw shock power. With its crushing fist, heavy lasers, and layered autoguns, it thrives in brutal close-quarters fighting where finesse is meaningless and only overwhelming force decides the outcome.

### Overlord `OVR-L1`

The Overlord was created as a command mech for Protectorate strike groups, its size and armament meant to dominate attention. Though later fielded in greater numbers, its reputation as a battlefield overseer still clings to it.

### Berserker `BER-Z3`

The Berserker channels raw ferocity into every engagement. Its crushing fist and banks of medium lasers let it drive headlong into enemy lines, while smoke rounds shroud its reckless advance. It is feared less for precision than for the chaos it unleashes up close.

### Thunder God `TDR-G7`

The Thunder God takes its name from its battlefield presence—heavy cannons and vulkans that echo like storms breaking over the horizon. Protectorate lore holds that its arrival signals the final hammerblow of a campaign.

### Infernal `INF-L9`

The Infernal’s earliest deployments left entire valleys scorched black, and its fire-spewing design cemented its apocalyptic reputation. Protectorate commanders often use it as a psychological weapon as much as a physical one.

### Doomhammer `DOOM-H2`

The Doomhammer was first forged to break stone bunkers and hardened enemy positions. With massive vulkans and crushing fists, it still fulfills that grim role, delivering hammerblows of smoke and fire to erase dug-in resistance.

### Brute Machine `BRT-100R`

A product of rushed wartime design, the Brute Machine was derided for its inelegance but proved itself by sheer weight of fire. Its survival and adoption are proof of the Protectorate’s core belief: overwhelming force wins wars.

### Cromagnon `CRM-N2`

The Cromagnon’s name came from rival houses mocking its primitive simplicity—until Protectorate pilots smashed through their most advanced fortifications. Now it is worn with pride, a blunt instrument that proves sophistication isn’t always required.

### Juggernaut `JGR-N6`

The second heaviest mech ever fielded in the Core, the Juggernaut was first deployed to break the sieges of the Border Wars, where its unstoppable advance became legend. With layered armor, a crushing fist, and a mix of rockets, lasers, and autoguns, it grinds forward until resistance collapses. Entire fortresses have fallen simply by being in its path.

### Ragnarok `RGK-5000K`

The largest mech ever fielded, the Ragnarok is the end made metal. A terrifying opponent in close ranges, it is heavily armed with vulkans, cannons, and scorcher systems and turns battlefields into apocalyptic wastelands. Wherever it marches, smoke and ruin follow, sealing the fate of all who oppose the Protectorate.

### Zephyr `ZPH-01`

A relic of the Imperium’s dueling traditions, the Zephyr was designed for blinding speed, able to outpace heavier mechs with ease. Armed with a blaster and light lasers, it darts in, strikes, and vanishes, leaving foes bewildered. Its name speaks to its nature: swift as wind, impossible to grasp.

### Drake `DRK-06`

The Drake was crafted in homage to the Imperium’s dragon symbology, its blaster and twin scorchers breathing fire across enemy lines. Lightly armored but terrifying up close, it thrives in ambushes and raids. Pilots often paint their machines with scaled patterns, reinforcing its image as a dragon reborn in steel.

### Ronin `RON-12`

A mech for warriors without masters, the Ronin was adopted by Imperium mercenary bands during the Old Imperium. With rockets for sudden fury and a blaster for dueling, it embodies the lone fighter’s creed: self-reliance, defiance, and a readiness to sell its blade to any cause.

### Pyrefox `PYX-53`

True to its name, the Pyrefox engulfs battlefields with blasters and fire weapons, making it a terror in close combat. Developed to cleanse rebellious enclaves, it carries both symbolic and practical weight—reminding enemies that resisting the Imperium will always end in flames.

### Incursor `ICR-99`

A reckless raider’s machine, the Incursor is fast, lightly protected, and dangerously over-armed for its frame. Rockets, blasters, and lasers make it unpredictable and deadly in the hands of bold pilots. Many Imperium aces built their reputations in Incursors, treating each battle as both gamble and performance.

### Jaguar `JGR-72`

Named for the fierce predator, the Jaguar embodies speed and ferocity. Its vulkan cannon and blaster combination allows it to tear through lighter foes, while its design prioritizes the swift, lethal strike over defense. In Imperium culture, Jaguars are seen as hunters born for war, and this mech carries that tradition.

### Ocelot `OCE-45`

### Umbrantarch `UMB-39`

### Redeemer `RDM-10`

### Nagara `NGA-47`

Built during an era of holy wars, the Nagara combined heavy firepower with symbolic weight. With its HEC and heavy Vulkan cannon, it was designed to break enemy champions and restore order. To this day, the Imperium frames Nagaras as machines of salvation through fire and steel.

### Drakkon `DRK-82`

A monstrous war machine, the Drakkon is drenched in flamethrowers and blasters, spewing destruction in every direction. It earned its name not from myth, but from its battlefield presence—unyielding, terrifying, and impossible to ignore. To the Imperium, the Drakkon is both dragon and executioner.

### Ordosmech `ORD-07`

The Ordosmech embodies the Imperium’s martial faith, built as a crusader’s machine. Armed with Vulkan cannons, rockets, and blasters, it balances mass firepower with religious symbolism. ### Warclaw `WCL-61`

The Warclaw was commissioned during the Imperium’s early expansion campaigns, when rapid assaults proved insufficient against fortified resistance. Designed for prolonged engagements, it combines blasters, Vulkan cannons, and scorcher systems to apply constant pressure while advancing methodically. The Warclaw is deployed when territory must be taken and held, not merely breached.

### Nightwing `NGT-W4`

The Nightwing prowls battlefields like a shadow, unleashing rockets, flamethrowers, and blaster fire before fading back into darkness. Heavily armed for its size, it embodies the Imperium’s preference for intimidation as well as annihilation. Its name, like its silhouette, evokes wings spread across the night sky.

### Onagor `ONA-37`

### Praetorian `PRA-97`

The Praetorian is one of the Imperium’s most feared and recognizable war machines. ### Neomech `NEO-60`

### Star Slayer `SLY-10`

### Photon `PHN-50T`

Developed by the Sentinel Program under the Orion Standards Commission, the Photon was the first widely fielded scout with projected shielding. Its medium prismatic array doubles as a sensor-liaison tool, letting it tag targets and survive brushes that would scrap other lights.

### Tachyon `TCY-2R`

An offshoot of the Photon II trials, the Tachyon prioritized velocity and precision fire for counter-raider screens. Twin light and one medium prismatic give it sharp, repeatable bursts, while its avionics tie directly into early-warning buoys. On border nets, “tachyon alert” means a fast mover has already cut the angle.

### Zeno `ZEN-400P`

Created for medium-range interdiction, the Zeno pairs a medium prismatic with economical rocket salvos to wear down fleeing targets. Modular hardpoints and easy field service made it a favorite of Citizen Guard patrols. Its name nods to pursuit paradoxes—once a Zeno starts the chase, the gap never seems to close for the prey.

### Darius `DR-300S`

Commissioned by the Heavy Systems Bureau to give citizen brigades a dependable lineholder, the Darius blends twin medium prismatics with stabilizing shields and guided missiles. It lacks flourish but excels at steady positional warfare, advancing behind a rational wall of data and fire. Crews respect it for what it is: the dependable center of an orderly advance.

### Cobalt `CLT-B1`

A precision striker from the Alloy Labs Initiative, the Cobalt mates a heavy energy cannon to a medium prismatic under reinforced shields. Its distinctive blue ceramic laminate isn’t for show—it dissipates heat and mitigates spall. Graduation units from several academies award “Cobalt keys” to pilots certified on its demanding fire-control regimen.

### Watcher `WAT-002`

Designed as a mesh-linked sentry, the Watcher stitches missiles, rockets, and prismatics into the battlespace network, shaping enemy movement rather than chasing it. When a Watcher is present, commanders talk about lanes and closures—the field becomes a diagram, and the diagram favors Orion.

### Deviance `DEV-2N`

The Deviance began as an unsanctioned field refit, where pilots overloaded a standard chassis with heavy prismatic arrays and rockets beyond recommended tolerances. Though the Assembly initially condemned the design, its brutal effectiveness in trials forced acceptance. Its name stuck, a reminder that sometimes progress comes from breaking rules.

### Xerxes `XER-200A`

Part of the Archives’ “Kings Series” naming convention, the Xerxes is an expeditionary spearhead with twin heavy prismatics, rockets, and resilient shields. It isn’t subtle—its job is to impose order at the point of contact. Citizen brigades treat it like a mobile verdict: the argument ends where Xerxes stands.

### Echelon `ECH-4R`

Built for charter enforcement and clean disables, the Echelon uses paired light prismatics and mixed missiles to peel weapons without catastrophic kills. Its telemetry suite prioritizes limb and mount solutions over cores. Echelons are routinely assigned to interdiction details, where compliance is required intact rather than destroyed.

### Blaze Bringer `BLZ-360K`

A field-illumination and suppression platform, the Blaze Bringer throws a curtain of coherent light—medium and light prismatics that dazzle sensors and scour cover. High sink capacity lets it sustain the show long after others cool off. Night operations doctrine calls it “turning darkness to glass.”

### Reckoner `REK-4M`

The Reckoner serves as an anchor and command relay. Heavy and medium prismatics handle threat nodes while a rocket bank shapes approaches. Its hardened comms and fire-control links allow it to coordinate allied units under sustained contact. Once established, a Reckoner position is intended to be held, not redeployed.

### Eradicator `ERAD-120R`

Despite the severe name, the Eradicator is a surgical siege tool. A heavy energy cannon and twin prismatics cut precise breach patterns while rockets clear debris and counter-pushes. City engineers like working behind it; the hole it makes is the hole the plan expected.

### Zealot `ZEL-20T`

Adopted enthusiastically by Citizen Guard auxiliaries, the Zealot layers shields and a full prismatic suite to hold ground with conviction. Rockets punish flanking attempts while heavy and medium beams keep pressure steady. Its “zeal” isn’t mysticism—just the civic habit of not giving an inch.

### Endermech `END-550M`

The Endermech serves as a versatile line-breaker, protected by advanced shielding. Armed with a heavy energy cannon, twin heavy prismatics, and missile support, it can adapt to almost any battlefield role. Pilots often remark on its uncanny precision, as if the machine anticipates threats a moment before they emerge.

### Markov `MKV-60L`

The Markov integrates predictive targeting from the Markov Kernel Project with twin heavy prismatics and deep heat reserves. Pilots joke it only needs to lock once; after that the future is already computed. In analytics drills, its variance charts are a thing of austere beauty.

### Rift Master `RFM-220C`

The Rift Master bends battlefields to its will. A chokepoint dominator, it seals narrow passes with heavy prismatic fire and rocket storms. Its shield lattice is tuned for glancing shots from extreme angles—perfect for canyon walls and megastructure corridors.

### Nebuchadnezzar `NEB-Z001`

A prestige fortress platform from the Archives’ “City-Builder” lineage, the Nebuchadnezzar layers heavy, medium, and light prismatics with compliant missiles under deep shields. It’s meant to hold capitals and corridors, projecting calm inevitability. The name fits: it builds order by unbuilding resistance.

### Aeon `AE-550X`

The Aeon is the pinnacle of Corporate technology, a mech designed to embody timeless supremacy. Quad heavy prismatics gave it unrivaled firepower, while advanced shields can make it feel invincible. Corporate propaganda has declared it ‘the machine that will outlast eternity.’
