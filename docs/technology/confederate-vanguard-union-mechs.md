# Union Mechs

Union mech forces combine widely available Core designs with heavy industrial frames built for mass deployment, durability, and sustained mechanized assault.

This roster includes both the common Core designs available to all major factions and the signature designs most closely associated with the Confederate Vanguard Union.

## Common Core Designs

### Hornet `HN-120`

One of the oldest light designs still in circulation, the Hornet was cheap to manufacture and simple to maintain. Armed with twin light lasers, it was never meant to duel, but rather to scout, harass, and sting at vulnerable points before fleeing. Its proliferation across the Core is owed to its reliability and low cost—where there is conflict, Hornets are never far behind.

### Lynx `LNX-40E`

The Lynx became popular during the border wars for its balance of mobility and firepower. With paired medium lasers and a small rocket rack, it could serve as both flanker and support unit. Though newer mechs have surpassed it, the Lynx endures thanks to its rugged frame and versatility—qualities that made it a staple in every House’s arsenal.

### Outrider `OR-640`

Built for patrol duty and frontier policing, the Outrider combined endurance with straightforward weaponry. Its heavy and medium lasers gave it enough punch to deter raiders without requiring complex supply lines. While unimpressive by modern standards, the Outrider’s long service life and adaptability have ensured its presence in garrisons across the Core.

### Yeoman `YM-10F`

The Yeoman was designed as a dependable multi-role platform for Houses lacking specialized production. Its mixed loadout—autoguns, lasers, and scorchers—made it a jack-of-all-trades capable of handling infantry, armor, or other mechs in a pinch. Though rarely a commander’s first choice, the Yeoman’s steady performance earned it a reputation as the soldier’s friend.

### Lineman `LM-30C`

Heavy, slow, and built to endure punishment, the Lineman was conceived as a frontline brawler to hold the line while others maneuvered. Its vulkan cannon and paired autoguns provided steady suppressive fire. Though outclassed by newer heavies, it remains common wherever Houses need durable firepower to anchor defenses.

### Lancer `LNC-220`

The Lancer was among the first medium-heavies to emphasize precision energy fire over massed rockets. Armed with a heavy laser and layered support beams, it excelled at punching through weakened armor. Once hailed as a duelist’s machine, it is now more often used as a training platform or second-line support, though many veteran pilots still prize its accuracy.

### Longstrike `LG-210`

An aging missile platform, the Longstrike was originally deployed to provide sustained bombardment from behind the lines. With its paired racks and autogun backups, it could soften enemy positions before an assault. Though slow and vulnerable up close, its simplicity and ease of production have kept it in steady circulation across the Core.

### Champion `CH-70K`

Once built to serve in ceremonial tournaments as well as war, the Champion balanced missile fire with lasers and a vulkan. Though never as specialized as later duelists, it gained popularity as a reliable all-rounder. Many Houses still field Champions in honor guards or training battalions, where their long heritage and recognizable silhouette command respect.

### Bulldog `BD-770`

The Bulldog earned its name in countless sieges, battering defenses with high energy cannons and rocket salvos. Slow but stubborn, it excelled in drawn-out attrition battles. Even today, the Bulldog remains a favored choice for militias and second-line forces who need a hard-hitting mech that can take punishment and keep advancing.

### Marksman `MK-350`

The Marksman was designed as a fire-support chassis, mounting multiple vulkans for sustained barrages. Though criticized for its heat inefficiency and limited armor, it carved a niche as a battlefield “sweeper,” punishing light mechs and infantry that strayed too close. In modern times, it is often relegated to defensive roles, but its name still carries weight.

### Gladiator `GL-882`

A relic of the arena era, the Gladiator was built for spectacle as much as war. With rockets, vulkan, and lasers, it was capable of delivering flashy, punishing blows that made it a crowd favorite. Many Houses retired theirs to ceremonial duty, but in poorer regions of the Core, Gladiators still see combat, proving that even showpieces can draw blood.

### Kingsguard `KG-950`

The Kingsguard was once marketed as the ultimate “command mech,” a prestige unit to accompany noble lords into battle. Its missiles and mixed weaponry made it flexible, while its armor kept its pilot secure. Though time has dulled its reputation, the Kingsguard endures as a symbol of authority on the battlefield—still favored by militia leaders and House scions alike.


## Signature Union Designs

### Raptor `RPT-1R`

Developed as a fast-response raider during the Union’s early frontier wars, the Raptor sacrifices heavy armor for speed and strike capability. Its rocket pods and light lasers made it ideal for harassing supply lines and crippling softer targets before heavier Union assets arrived. Though lightly protected, pilots trusted its agility and ferocity—earning the name Raptor for its swift, predatory strikes.

### Vagabond `VG-7H`

The Vagabond was born of necessity during the Exile, when scattered Union units required a versatile scout and skirmisher. Outfitted with jump jets and a mixed ballistic-energy loadout, it excels in rough terrain where heavier mechs cannot tread. Its name reflects both its wandering origins and its reputation as a survivor, roaming far from supply lines and returning with kills to prove its worth.

### Slugger `SLG-1A`

Designed for brutal city fighting during the Restoration, the Slugger carries a heavy energy cannon backed by close-support lasers. Slow but unyielding, it was built to clear enemy strongpoints building by building. The name Slugger is both a nod to its hammering firepower and the Union’s reputation for winning through attrition, no matter how long the fight drags on.

### Alpha Wolf `AWLF-5K`

The Alpha Wolf represents the Union’s doctrine of coordinated pack warfare. Armed with paired autoguns, lasers, and missiles, it was designed to anchor wolfpack formations that overwhelmed isolated foes through relentless firepower. Feared for its ability to tear through both armor and morale, the Alpha Wolf earned its name by always leading the kill.

### Crossfire `CRF-0S`

Born from Union doctrine that no enemy should leave the field unscathed, the Crossfire bristles with overlapping weapon arcs—Vulkan cannon, rockets, lasers, and autogun. It was intended for siege and suppression, saturating kill zones until nothing remained. Pilots often joked that standing in its line of fire was like being caught between two armies—hence the name Crossfire.

### Firebrand `FR-3N`

The Firebrand’s origin lies in the Union’s scorched-earth campaigns. Armed with an array of Scorchers supported by lasers and autoguns, it was built to terrorize entrenched enemies, burning them from cover and leaving nothing but ash. The Union embraced the mech’s fiery symbolism, fielding it as both weapon and warning: resistance would be answered with flames.

### Dragoon `DGN-8S`

The Dragoon was designed as a flexible heavy line unit, able to hold or advance with equal confidence. Combining a heavy energy cannon, vulkan, and defensive lasers, it proved especially valuable in prolonged campaigns where reliability outweighed elegance. Its name recalls the mounted infantry of old—soldiers who could fight wherever they were needed, a tradition the Union holds dear.

### Maverick `MAV-8K`

The Maverick was developed for commanders who demanded independence from supply trains. Its rockets and high energy cannon made it a devastating ambush platform, while its jump jets allowed it to strike from unexpected angles. The name Maverick honors both its battlefield role and the defiant Union spirit of breaking from convention to seize victory.

### Jackman `JCK-77`

The Jackman was built during the urban reclamations, where Union forces needed a close-range brawler to clear streets and hold chokepoints. Armed with paired vulkans and heavy lasers, it is devastating in knife-fight ranges. The name reflects the laborers who “jackhammered” the Union’s cities back into shape, embodying relentless industrial grit turned to war.

### Patriot `PTR-007`

The Patriot is perhaps the most symbolic of Union mechs, a walking fortress embodying their doctrine of sacrifice and resilience. With thick armor, broad weapon coverage, and missile support, it became the backbone of ceremonial as well as battlefield formations. Its name was no accident—the Union intended it as a rallying banner, a machine that stood unbroken while others fell.

### Hammerhead `HMR-4T`

Developed for shipboard and port assaults, the Hammerhead mounts twin heavy energy cannons backed by autoguns and lasers. Its name derives from its brutal frontal assault style, “hammering” through defenses like the predator of the sea. Pilots often describe it as inelegant but unstoppable—exactly the qualities the Union prizes.

### Challenger `CHL-4R`

The Challenger was created during the Union’s dueling trials, where mechs were fielded to prove dominance over rival Houses. Mixing rockets, autoguns, and lasers, it was designed for versatility in single combat as well as battlefield challenges. Though less specialized than some designs, its name and legacy are steeped in Union pride: to challenge is to overcome.

### Brigadier `BRG-88R`

Built to lead batteries of missile carriers, the Brigadier became the cornerstone of Union siege doctrine. Capable of drowning entrenched foes in waves of rockets and missiles, it earned a reputation for ending battles before they truly began. The name Brigadier reflects its command role—always at the center of Union formations, directing fire and breaking lines.

### Bulwark `BLW-5K`

Designed as a defensive anchor, the Bulwark carries massive armor and layered weaponry suited for holding ground against superior numbers. In campaigns where the Union was forced to defend its hard-won territories, the Bulwark became both shield and hammer. Its name speaks for itself: immovable, unshakable, a wall of steel.

### Sword Breaker `SBRK-7R`

The Sword Breaker was engineered specifically to counter the dueling champions of rival Houses. With heavy vulkans and precision medium lasers, it excelled at stripping weapons from enemy mechs and leaving them powerless. Its name is both literal and symbolic—the Union’s message that no foreign blade, however sharp, could pierce their resolve.

### Punisher `PNR-004`

The Punisher was born from campaigns of retribution. With a heavy energy cannon, ultra vulkan, and missile support, it was built to chase down and annihilate those who dared defy Union authority. Slow but merciless, the Punisher lives up to its name, serving as executioner on the battlefield where no mercy is given.

### Gallimagus `GLM-8G`

The Gallimagus is an ancient design, said to descend from pre-Exile Empire prototypes. Mounting an unprecedented four heavy energy cannons, it was rarely produced due to cost, but every unit became a battlefield legend. Its name, drawn from old Union sagas, evokes a monstrous beast of war—rare, mythical, and terrifying in its wrath.

### Liberator `LBR-9D`

Developed during the Long Exile by remnants of the fallen Empire, the Liberator was designed to spearhead counteroffensives and break enemy sieges. It played a decisive role in the Great Restoration, leading the charge to reclaim the Core worlds. Adopted as the Union’s premiere assault mech, it endures as both battlefield powerhouse and enduring symbol of liberation.
