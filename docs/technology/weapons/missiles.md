# Missiles

> *"A rocket hits where you fire it. A missile hits where you meant to fire it."*
>
> — Sovereignty artillery officer

## Overview

Missile Launchers are long-range guided weapon systems designed to engage targets beyond the reach of most direct-fire weapons.

Unlike Rocket Pods, which rely upon large salvos of semi-guided projectiles, missiles employ sophisticated guidance systems capable of tracking and adjusting course throughout flight. This allows missile-equipped units to engage moving targets, strike from beyond visual range, and coordinate attacks using information provided by scouts, sensors, and allied forces.

Their exceptional reach has made missiles one of the most important fire-support weapons in modern mech warfare.

---

## Battlefield Role

Missiles specialize in long-range engagement and battlefield support.

A missile launcher can threaten enemy units at distances where most weapons cannot effectively respond. Combined with modern sensor networks, this allows missile-equipped formations to engage targets that are hidden, maneuvering, or operating beyond direct line of sight. Many missile systems are capable of indirect fire, allowing them to strike enemies concealed behind terrain or other obstacles when targeting information is available.

Unlike rockets, missiles concentrate their damage into fewer but more powerful impacts. This makes them particularly effective against exposed internal systems and vulnerable components once armor has been breached. Missile strikes frequently cause critical damage disproportionate to their apparent size.

Missile-equipped units are commonly positioned behind the front line where they can provide continuous fire support while remaining protected from direct attack. Their ability to influence engagements across vast distances makes them valuable force multipliers in both offensive and defensive operations.

The weapon's primary disadvantages are cost, ammunition requirements, and slow reload cycles. Missile launchers typically fire less frequently than most other weapon systems, making every salvo important. They also perform poorly at extremely close range, where guidance systems have insufficient time to properly acquire and maneuver toward their targets.

For this reason, missile warfare is often closely tied to reconnaissance assets, sensor platforms, aerospace support, and electronic warfare systems.

## History

Modern missile systems emerged from centuries of advances in guidance technology, sensor integration, and battlefield communications.

While early missile weapons were little more than self-propelled explosives, modern launchers employ sophisticated targeting packages capable of tracking targets across vast distances. As communications networks improved throughout the Core, missile warfare became increasingly integrated with reconnaissance, command, and sensor systems.

This evolution transformed missiles from simple weapons into components of larger battlefield networks. A missile launched by one mech may ultimately strike a target identified by a scout several kilometers away.

Most major powers field missile-equipped formations, though the Starcrest Protectorate remains a notable exception. Protectorate military doctrine traditionally emphasizes direct engagement, aggressive maneuver, and close-range combat, leaving little room for the stand-off warfare that missile systems encourage.

Today, missiles remain among the most feared weapons on the battlefield. A pilot may never see the mech responsible for launching the salvo that destroys them.
